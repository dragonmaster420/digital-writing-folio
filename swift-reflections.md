**Week 1**

I’d define ‘E-Literature’ as any kind of writing that has to be read digitally to fully experience it. Like I wouldn’t say it includes e-books, since they’re digitised versions of text that is already or could otherwise be in print form. Anything that’s generative or playable or features stuff like pictures moving wouldn’t be the same if it was. I do think what I understand to be e-literature will change across the unit once I start branching out into more unconventional work.

I got into Shade, which I think relies on the digital medium the most out of all the pieces. That’s what made it the most endearing to me—it was pretty much like playing a game. I appreciated, as well, that despite how user-driver it is, the creator managed to maintain a really clear creative voice. What really makes it stand out from more standard literature was that there’s an element of difficulty to it, in that users have to do a lot of trial and error to progress, which there isn’t necessarily in the linear experience of reading a book. So while the lows were definitely lower, the satisfaction I got from picking the right option and progressing made the highs higher, and gave me a sense of a more personal achievement.

I'm really keen on e-literature as a means to present stories in new ways. You can be more ambitions with visual elements and use a wider scope in terms of technology, but also use certain tools to make what you're trying to communicate get across better. Reader interactivity is another exciting avenue. Seems like digital stuff could be more engaging and facilitate a kind of collaboration between author and reader. Like my playthrough of Shade is a text in itself, and would differ from basically everyone else's.


**Week 2**

I do get what the people Beres is referencing mean in saying that Instagram poetry is “indivisible” from most stuff on social media, but honestly, I find the preexisting division of ‘literature’ from all other writing a bit bullshit.

In terms of the definition of e-literature, I find it alright to call a piece that’s interactive and thought-provoking or whatever that, but the inverse of excluding another because it isn’t rubs me the wrong way. I don’t think anyone would hesitate to say that poetry is ‘literature’, but suddenly the fact that it’s on a platform as diverse and accessible as Instagram makes it not. Kind of elitist, in my opinion.

I agree with the advocates for 'literature', to an extent, that there are heaps of people who repost or even write their own ‘Instapoetry’ so they’ll come off as well-read or intelligent, and that that commodification does take away from the alleged ‘purpose’ of art. But that doesn’t go for everyone. I’d say lot of peoples’ first readings of poetry were on social media, and that consigning anything like that to not being on the same level as this supposedly elevated e-literature comes across as gatekeeping. I think someone who found Yeats or whatever online has as much of a right to his work than someone who found him in a book.

The term ‘non-trivial effort’ in reference to e-literature is part of its proposed definition. It’s stated that in order for something to be e-literature, it must ask the ‘reader’ to engage with it in a way that isn’t, I guess, how someone would read “populist bestsellers” as Beres words Aarseth and Hayles’ positions on the matter. I find all of that too broad a statement. To me, it’s more academics shitting on casual audiences.

I’m not having a go at e-literature. Reading stuff like this gets a rise out of me regardless of what it’s dealing with.


**Week 3**

I obviously use technology every fucking day of my life. Even right now, I’m writing this on a Microsoft Word document on my laptop, reading the briefing for it off an internet tab. Right after this, I’m likely going to boot up my PS4 and watch Married at First Sight while I do so. That show’s for sure the marker of capitalist-consumerist waste in modern TV, but it’s my Soylent Green, man.

I do have issues with state of the internet right now, especially in the way that Triscari brings up this lamenting of what it used to be. I’m semi-aware that nostalgia may be getting in the way of me making a proper comparison, but I can’t stop myself from feeling that, can I?

I’m on this thing at the moment where I’m going back to a lot of interests I had before I grew up and dismissed them as cringe. I associate stuff like my past obsession with the fantasy genre and early 2010s Nintendo media with an iteration of the internet that was nothing but fascination and excitement for me. That kind of golden age of memes that eventually became the ‘MLG’ era was what I was raised on, and I remember old YouTube and game forums as places where people were so keen to share their interests and put other people onto them, building communities where they actually made friends.

Since most of my writing now is set in the present and centres on the young adulthood that I’m in, it’s practically impossible not to have technology as this constant presence. Text message conversations, dating apps, social media profiles.

I’ve recently gotten into Substack as a means of sharing short stories I’m writing, too. I’m finding myself getting sort of cynical, having similar issues with it that critics of ‘Instapoetry’ have. But it’s hard to know, through the screen, who’s putting it on for the sake of appearances and who genuinely wants to write.

But I also don’t like how immediately sceptical I am. I feel like that’s a by-product of a lot of this ‘post-Web’ stuff.
